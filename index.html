Chapter 12 — Muscle Contraction

Three muscle types, the structure of skeletal muscle down to the myofilament, the cross-bridge cycle, and how stimulation triggers contraction and relaxation.

Three muscle types

  • Skeletal: striated, has sarcomeres, multinucleated, voluntary.
  • Cardiac: has actin & myosin in sarcomeres, contracts by sliding-filament; cells joined by gap junctions, impulses spread through them so it behaves as one unit (functional syncytium).
  • Smooth: no sarcomeres; myofilaments attach to dense bodies at the ends of the cell; has gap junctions. Contraction depends on a rise in free intracellular Ca²⁺; relaxes when Ca²⁺ falls.

Skeletal muscle structure

  • Muscle → hundreds of muscle fibers → threadlike myofibrils → myofilaments (thick & thin). Striations come from overlapping filaments.
  • The muscle cell = muscle fiber; plasma membrane with inward extensions (T tubules) = sarcolemma. Many nuclei & mitochondria (sarcosome).
  • Sarcoplasm contains glycogen, ATP, phosphocreatine, glycolytic enzymes.
  • Sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) stores calcium.

The sarcomere

  • Basic unit, connected end-to-end by Z-lines.
  • A band: dark band. I band: light band between ends of myosin. H zone: area where the two ends of the thin filaments fail to meet. Z-line: separates sarcomeres.
  • Thick filament = myosin. Thin filament = actin + troponin + tropomyosin.

Myofilament proteins

  • Actin: forms cross bridges with myosin during contraction (has active/binding sites).
  • Tropomyosin: sits in the groove of actin and blocks myosin-binding sites.
  • Troponin complex (3 polypeptides): Troponin T binds tropomyosin; Troponin I inhibits actin–myosin interaction when Ca²⁺ is absent; Troponin C binds calcium.
  • Myosin head contains ATPase and binds ATP.

Cross-bridge cycle / muscle contraction

  • Myosin splits ATP → ADP + Pi (both stay bound until myosin attaches to actin).
  • Pi released → power stroke → pulls actin toward the center of the A band.
  • ADP released as myosin binds a fresh ATP at the end of the power stroke.
  • Binding of a new ATP breaks the cross-bridge bond → cross bridges detach, ready to rebind.
  • Sliding filament theory: filaments slide past each other; sarcomere shortens (thick/thin lengths don't change).

Motor unit stimulation (excitation-contraction)

  • Action potential travels down a motor neuron to the neuromuscular junction.
  • Neuron releases acetylcholine into the synaptic cleft → binds receptors on the muscle fiber.
  • AP travels down the T tubules → depolarization opens Ca²⁺ channels in the SR → Ca²⁺ released.
  • Ca²⁺ binds troponin → shape change pushes tropomyosin off the active sites → exposes myosin-binding sites → cross bridges form (after ATP → ADP + Pi).

Muscle relaxation

  • Nerve stimulation must cease.
  • Ca²⁺ pumped back into the SR by Ca²⁺-ATPase.
  • Ca²⁺ leaves troponin C → tropomyosin re-covers the binding sites → actin–myosin interaction ends → relaxation.

Chapter 16 — Respiratory System

System zones, the four processes of respiration, the mechanics of ventilation (pressures, Boyle's law), lung properties, volumes & capacities, gas transport, and control of breathing.

Zones & functions

  • Conducting zone: conducts air; warms, humidifies, filters and cleans it (mucus traps particles, cilia move mucus up). Air reaches the respiratory zone at 37 °C, saturated with water vapor.
  • Respiratory zone: site of gas exchange — respiratory bronchioles, alveolar sacs, alveoli.
  • Gas exchange occurs entirely by diffusion — rapid due to huge surface area + short diffusion distance.

Four processes of respiration

  • Pulmonary ventilation (breathing): moves air in/out.
  • External respiration: gas exchange lungs ↔ blood.
  • Transport of gases by blood.
  • Internal respiration: gas exchange blood ↔ tissues (= cellular respiration / O₂ utilization).

Pleura & thoracic cavity

  • Each lung is in a closed pleural sac. Parietal pleura lines thoracic wall & diaphragm; visceral pleura attaches to the lung.
  • A thin layer of intrapleural fluid (a few mL) lubricates; its surface tension resists separation, so the lungs cling to the thorax wall.

Pressures & Boyle's law

  • Law #1: air flows by bulk flow from high to low pressure.
  • Atmospheric pressure (Patm) at sea level = 760 mmHg = 1 atm. Zero respiratory pressure = atmospheric.
  • Intrapulmonary (Ppul): pressure in alveoli; rises & falls with breathing and always equalizes with Patm.
  • Boyle's law: at constant T, P varies inversely with V (P₁V₁ = P₂V₂). ↑lung volume → ↓pressure → air in; ↓volume → ↑pressure → air out.

Inspiration vs expiration

  • Inspiration = active. Diaphragm contracts (↑volume vertically) + external intercostals raise ribs (↑volume laterally). Ppul drops 0 → −3 mmHg; Patm > Ppul → air in. ("Negative pressure breathing" = pulling air in.)
  • Quiet expiration = passive (depends on lung elasticity/recoil). Ppul rises −3 → +3 mmHg; Ppul > Patm → air out.
  • Forced expiration = active (abdominal wall muscles). Forced inspiration uses accessory muscles.

Physical properties of the lungs

  • Compliance: distensibility = ΔV / ΔP. Reduced by pulmonary fibrosis.
  • Elasticity: tendency to return to original size (high elastin content); recoil drives expiration.
  • Surface tension: fluid film in alveoli resists distension and tends to collapse the alveolus.
  • Surfactant: secreted by type II alveolar cells (phospholipids + proteins); lowers surface tension, works better as alveoli shrink → prevents alveolar collapse during expiration.

  • Lung volumes assessed by spirometry. TLC depends on age, sex, height.
  • Anatomical dead space: air that doesn't reach beyond terminal bronchioles (no gas exchange).

Restrictive vs obstructive

  • Restrictive (e.g. pulmonary fibrosis): normal airway resistance, impaired movement; vital capacity reduced.
  • Obstructive (e.g. asthma, bronchitis): VC usually normal but expiration is slower/harder. Diagnosed by rate of expiration.
  • FEV1: healthy people exhale ~80% of VC in 1 sec. FEV1/FVC < 70% indicates obstruction (COPD/asthma).

Gas partial pressures & transport

Systemic veins / pulmonary arteriesPO₂ 40, PCO₂ 46 mmHg
Systemic arteries / pulmonary veinsPO₂ 100, PCO₂ 40 mmHg
  • O₂ transport: ~98.5% bound to hemoglobin, ~1.5% dissolved in plasma. Oxyhemoglobin = HbO₂; deoxyhemoglobin = HHb. Arterial O₂ saturation ≈ 97%.
  • CO₂ transport (3 forms): dissolved (~1/10), carbaminohemoglobin (~1/5), and bicarbonate HCO₃⁻ (most). Carbonic acid forms mostly in RBCs.

Control of breathing & acid–base

  • Rhythm set by the medulla oblongata: VRG (rhythm generator, ~12–15 breaths/min) and DRG (integrates stretch & chemoreceptor input). Pontine centers smooth the transitions.
  • Regulated by feedback on arterial PO₂, pH, PCO₂. Medulla uses tissue-fluid pH as an index of blood CO₂. Central chemoreceptors in medulla; peripheral near aorta & carotid arteries.
  • Normal arterial pH 7.35–7.45 (avg 7.40); main buffer = bicarbonate. Lungs regulate CO₂, kidneys regulate HCO₃⁻.
  • Acidosis < 7.35, Alkalosis > 7.45. Respiratory acidosis = hypoventilation (↑CO₂); respiratory alkalosis = hyperventilation.

Nervous System Physiology — Neuronal Signaling

Organization of the nervous system, neuron structure & classification, membrane potentials, action potentials & propagation, synapses & neurotransmitters, and the autonomic nervous system.

Organization

  • CNS: brain + spinal cord. PNS: 12 pairs cranial nerves + 31 pairs spinal nerves.
  • Two cell types: Neurons (conduct impulses) and supporting/neuroglial cells (feed, support, accelerate).

Neuron structure & classification

  • Cell body (perikaryon): integration/nutrition center. Bodies cluster in nuclei (CNS) and ganglia (PNS).
  • Dendrites: receptive area, carry signals toward the cell body. Axon: conducting region, carries impulses away; AP starts at the axon hillock; terminals are the secretory region (release neurotransmitters).
  • Functional types: Sensory/afferent (receptors → CNS), Motor/efferent (CNS → effectors), Interneurons (entirely within CNS, integrative).

Membrane & resting potential

  • Resting membrane potential ≈ −70 mV (inside negative); steady potential of a cell not signaling.
  • Changing membrane permeability to an ion changes the potential. Excitability/irritability = ability to produce & conduct potential changes.
  • Depolarization (less negative, excitatory) ↑ chance of impulse; hyperpolarization (more negative, inhibitory) ↓ it. Repolarization = returning toward rest. Overshoot = inside becomes positive.

Graded vs action potentials

  • Graded potentials (dendrites): short distance, vary in size, decay with distance, via chemically-gated channels.
  • Action potentials (axons): long distance, don't decay, via voltage-gated Na⁺ & K⁺ channels.

Action potential (all-or-none)

  • Must reach threshold (~−55 / −50 mV) to fire; below it, nothing.
  • Depolarization: Na⁺ rushes in (Na⁺ permeability ~1000× resting). Repolarization: K⁺ moves out.
  • Peak doesn't exceed ~+30 mV because voltage-gated Na⁺ channels inactivate quickly and K⁺ channels open.
  • Na⁺/K⁺ pump restores ion distribution (Na⁺ out, K⁺ in). Amplitude & duration are fixed.

Propagation

  • Simple propagation: local currents depolarize the adjacent membrane forward (one direction); self-propagating at constant velocity.
  • Saltatory conduction: in myelinated neurons the impulse jumps between nodes of Ranvier (which hold the voltage-gated Na⁺/K⁺ channels) — much faster.

Synapse & neurotransmitters

  • Synapse: junction transferring info neuron→neuron or neuron→effector. Chemical synapses release neurotransmitters into the cleft.
  • NT action is short-lived; leftover NT is reabsorbed/recycled or broken down by enzymes.
  • Classes: Acetylcholine, monoamines, amino acids, peptides, purines, gases & lipids.
  • Monoamines: dopamine, epinephrine, norepinephrine, serotonin.
  • Excitatory amino acids: glutamate & aspartate (major excitatory in CNS). Inhibitory: GABA (from glutamic acid) & glycine.

PNS nerves

  • Cranial nerves (12 pairs): Sensory = 1, 2, 8; Motor = 3, 4, 6, 11, 12; Mixed = 5, 7, 9, 10.
  • Spinal nerves (31 pairs): 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal — all mixed.

Autonomic nervous system (ANS)

  • Controls involuntary effectors (cardiac & smooth muscle, glands). Uses a 2-neuron efferent pathway: preganglionic (cell body in CNS) → ganglion → postganglionic → target.
  • Main ANS neurotransmitters: acetylcholine (ACh) and norepinephrine (NE).
